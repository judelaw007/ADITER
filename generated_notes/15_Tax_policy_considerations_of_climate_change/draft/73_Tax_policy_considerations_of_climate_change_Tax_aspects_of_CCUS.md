# Chapter 73: Tax Aspects of CCUS

## 1. Introduction

Carbon Capture, Utilization and Storage (**CCUS**) technology represents a critical tool in achieving climate neutrality targets, particularly for hard-to-abate sectors such as cement, steel, and chemicals production. Given the substantial capital investment required for CCUS infrastructure—often exceeding USD 1 billion for large-scale facilities—tax policy plays a decisive role in determining project viability. This chapter examines the international tax framework supporting CCUS deployment, focusing on investment incentives, cost recovery mechanisms, and cross-border taxation challenges relevant to multinational energy companies.

## 2. Tax Incentives for CCUS Investment

### 2.1 Section 45Q Tax Credit (United States)

The Section 45Q tax credit, significantly enhanced by the Inflation Reduction Act 2022, provides the most generous CCUS incentive globally:

**Credit Structure (2025 values):**

| CO₂ Disposition | Base Credit | Direct Pay/Transfer |
|----------------|-------------|---------------------|
| Permanent geological storage | USD 85/tonne | Available |
| EOR with storage | USD 60/tonne | Available |
| Utilization | USD 60/tonne | Available |

**Key Features:**
- **Eligibility period**: 12 years from facility commencement
- **Phase-in inflation adjustment**: Credits indexed to inflation after 2027
- **Direct pay option**: Allows entities without tax liability to receive cash refunds (critical for joint ventures with tax-exempt partners)
- **Transferability**: Credits may be sold to unrelated third parties, creating new financing structures
- **Minimum capture thresholds**: 18,750 tonnes/year (direct air capture), 12,500 tonnes/year (other facilities)

**Tax Planning Considerations:**
1. **Partnership structures**: Allocation of 45Q credits among partners requires careful consideration of IRC §704(b) substantial economic effect rules
2. **Recapture provisions**: Credits subject to recapture if stored CO₂ leaks within three years
3. **Foreign tax credit interaction**: 45Q credits are specified credits under IRC §904(h), limiting foreign tax credit utilization

### 2.2 Investment Tax Credits and Accelerated Depreciation

**United Kingdom – Capital Allowances:**
- **First Year Allowances**: 100% super-deduction for qualifying CCUS equipment (available through March 2026)
- **Annual Investment Allowance**: £1 million annual allowance for plant and machinery
- **Long-life asset treatment**: CCUS pipelines and storage facilities qualify for 6% writing-down allowances

**Norway – Tax Depreciation:**
- **Immediate expensing**: CCUS equipment on Norwegian continental shelf eligible for 100% first-year depreciation under Petroleum Tax Act
- **Uplift allowance**: 24% risk allowance on uplifted basis (5.2% annually for four years)
- **Effective rate**: Combined 78% tax rate (56% special tax + 22% ordinary tax) makes depreciation timing highly valuable

**Canada – Investment Tax Credit (Budget 2024):**
- **Credit rate**: 37.5% for eligible CCUS equipment
- **Qualifying expenditures**: Capture, compression, pipeline transport, and storage infrastructure
- **Phase-out**: Credit reduces to 18.75% after 2030, eliminated by 2040

### 2.3 Operating Subsidies and Price Support

Several jurisdictions provide operational support beyond capital incentives:

**UK CCUS Cluster Sequencing Programme:**
- **Dispatchable Power Agreement (DPA)**: Revenue support for CCUS-equipped power generation
- **Industrial Carbon Capture Contract (ICC)**: Guaranteed price per tonne captured for industrial emitters
- **Tax treatment**: Payments taxable as ordinary income; taxpayers should evaluate whether payments constitute subsidies requiring basis reduction under CTA 2010

**Netherlands SDE++ Scheme:**
- **Subsidy structure**: Covers gap between CO₂ capture costs and ETS price
- **Tax implications**: Reduces deductible project costs; creates timing differences for deferred tax accounting

## 3. Transfer Pricing for CCUS Infrastructure

### 3.1 Shared Infrastructure Arrangements

CCUS projects frequently involve multiple parties sharing capture, transport, or storage infrastructure. Transfer pricing analysis must address:

**Cost Contribution Arrangements (CCAs):**
Under OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines Chapter VIII, CCAs allow multiple parties to share CCUS development costs and benefits:

- **Participant analysis**: Each participant must reasonably expect benefits commensurate with contributions
- **Allocation keys**: Common methods include captured CO₂ volume, proportional emissions, or reserved capacity
- **Buy-in/buy-out payments**: Required when participant joins existing infrastructure or exits arrangement

**Service Fee Arrangements:**
When one entity operates CCUS infrastructure for affiliated users:

- **Method selection**: Cost-plus method typically appropriate for routine infrastructure operation
- **Benchmarking challenges**: Limited comparable uncontrolled prices given nascent CCUS market
- **Risk allocation**: Operational risks, geological storage risks, and regulatory compliance risks must align with compensation

### 3.2 Intangible Property Considerations

CCUS technology development creates valuable intangibles requiring transfer pricing attention:

**Qualifying Intangibles:**
- Proprietary capture technology (e.g., novel solvents, membrane systems)
- Subsurface characterization data for storage sites
- Monitoring and verification methodologies

**DEMPE Analysis:**
Development, Enhancement, Maintenance, Protection, and Exploitation functions determine economic ownership under BEPS Actions 8-10:

- **Technology developer**: Retains returns associated with R&D risk-bearing
- **Licensed user**: Pays arm's-length royalty, typically 2-8% of avoided carbon costs or revenue enhanced by CCUS deployment
- **Cost-sharing**: Alternative structure where multiple parties jointly develop technology

## 4. International Tax Implications

### 4.1 Permanent Establishment Risk

**Pipeline Infrastructure:**
CCUS pipelines crossing international borders create PE exposure:

**OECD Model Article 5(1):**
- **Fixed place of business**: Pipeline route may constitute PE if taxpayer has right of use and business activities conducted through facility
- **Preparatory/auxiliary exception**: Article 5(4) unlikely to apply as CO₂ transport represents core business activity
- **Agency PE**: Third-party pipeline operators should be evaluated under Article 5(5) dependent agent rules

**Norway-UK Cross-Border Storage:**
Projects capturing CO₂ in UK and storing in Norwegian North Sea:
- **Norwegian taxing rights**: Storage activities likely create Norwegian PE subject to 78% marginal rate
- **Treaty relief**: Norway-UK treaty provides foreign tax credit relief for UK taxpayers
- **Documentation**: Maintain contemporaneous records of cross-border CO₂ movements for audit defense

### 4.2 Withholding Tax on Cross-Border Payments

**Service Fees:**
Payments for CCUS services to non-resident providers may trigger withholding:

- **Business profits article**: Treaty Article 7 exempts if no PE in source state
- **Technical services**: Some treaties (e.g., India, Brazil) impose withholding on technical service fees regardless of PE
- **Domestic law**: UK charges 20% withholding on "royalties or other sums" for IP use; carefully structure to avoid characterization as royalty

**Royalties for CCUS Technology:**
- **Treaty rates**: Typically 0-15% depending on bilateral treaty
- **Hybrid mismatches**: ATAD 2 denies deductions for payments not included in recipient's income; relevant for payments to hybrid entities in non-EU jurisdictions

### 4.3 Tax Credit Export and Monetization

**Section 45Q Credit Transferability:**
IRS Notice 2023-XX permits one-time credit transfers:

**Cross-Border Implications:**
- **Foreign buyers**: Non-US entities may purchase 45Q credits, but must have US tax liability to utilize
- **Withholding**: No withholding on credit sale proceeds (treated as tax attribute, not ordinary income)
- **Transfer pricing**: Related-party credit sales must reflect fair market value

**Analogous Structures:**
- **UK Contract for Difference**: CfD payments to foreign investors subject to 20% withholding unless treaty relief
- **Norwegian uplift**: Non-resident licensees may utilize uplift only against Norwegian petroleum income

## 5. Tax Accounting for CCUS Projects

### 5.1 Revenue Recognition – ASC 606

**45Q Credit Revenue:**
Performance obligation satisfied when CO₂ permanently stored (or utilized):

- **Transaction price**: Credit value per tonne indexed for inflation
- **Variable consideration**: Estimate credits subject to recapture using expected value method
- **Contract modification**: Direct pay election vs. credit transfer creates different accounting

**Subsidy Income (IFRS 15/IAS 20):**
Government grants for CCUS projects:

- **Recognition**: Income approach (recognize in P&L systematically) vs. offset approach (reduce asset cost)
- **Conditionality**: Income recognized when conditions reasonably certain to be met
- **Presentation**: Disclose accounting policy choice and grant amounts in financial statement notes

### 5.2 Deferred Tax – IAS 12

**Temporary Differences:**
Book-tax differences arise from:

1. **Accelerated tax depreciation**: Creates deductible temporary differences; recognize deferred tax assets
2. **Capitalized borrowing costs**: Different capitalization rules for tax vs. GAAP
3. **Government grants**: If offset against asset cost for tax but recognized as income for book, creates taxable temporary difference

**Example Calculation:**
CCUS facility with USD 500 million cost:
- **Tax basis**: USD 0 (100% first-year depreciation)
- **Book basis**: USD 450 million (after USD 50 million government grant offset)
- **Taxable temporary difference**: USD 450 million
- **Deferred tax liability**: USD 450 million × 21% = USD 94.5 million

## 6. State Aid and Subsidy Control

### 6.1 EU State Aid Rules (Pre-Brexit UK Projects)

CCUS subsidies must comply with Article 107 TFEU:

**General Block Exemption Regulation (GBER):**
Environmental aid for CCUS qualifies if:
- **Aid intensity**: Maximum 30% of eligible costs (45% for SMEs)
- **Eligible costs**: Additional investment costs vs. baseline technology
- **Notification threshold**: EUR 15 million per undertaking

**Important Environmental Projects (IPCEI):**
Large-scale CCUS projects may qualify under Article 107(3)(b):
- **Common European interest**: Cross-border CO₂ networks
- **Notification required**: Pre-clearance by European Commission
- **Tax implications**: State aid creates taxable income; recipient must evaluate whether EUR 200,000 de minimis exception applies

### 6.2 UK Subsidy Control Act 2022

Post-Brexit subsidy framework:

**Subsidy Control Principles:**
1. Pursue specific policy objective (climate change mitigation)
2. Proportionate and necessary
3. Minimize distortion to competition/investment
4. Transparency requirements

**Tax Subsidies:**
Enhanced capital allowances constitute subsidies requiring assessment under:
- **Prohibited subsidies**: Export subsidies, unlimited guarantees
- **Streamlined route**: Below GBP 315,000 over three years
- **Tax authority coordination**: HMRC must consult CMA for subsidies above GBP 10 million

### Solution Part 3: Credit Transfer Alternative

**Transfer Pricing Analysis:**

PetroCorp considers selling Year 1 credits (USD 120 million face value) to TechCo, a large technology company with substantial federal tax liability seeking to offset income.

**Market for 45Q Credits (2025):**
- **Observed transactions**: USD 0.90-0.95 per USD 1.00 face value
- **Discount factors**: Credit timing (1-year delay for IRS processing), counterparty risk, transfer complexity
- **Assume market price**: USD 0.92 per dollar

**Transfer Analysis:**

| Item | Direct Utilization | Credit Transfer |
|------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Face value (Year 1) | USD 120.0M | USD 120.0M |
| Cash received (Year 1) | - | USD 110.4M (120.0 × 0.92) |
| Tax benefit timing | Year 1 tax return | Immediate cash |
| PV of Year 1 credit @ 8% | USD 111.0M | USD 110.4M |
| Net advantage | USD 0.6M | - |

**Analysis:**
- **Direct utilization PV**: USD 120.0M / 1.08 = USD 111.0M (assuming credit utilized on Year 1 tax return filed in Year 2)
- **Transfer proceeds**: USD 110.4M received immediately
- **Marginal difference**: USD 0.6M favors direct utilization

**However, transfer may be preferable if:**
1. PetroCorp faces NOL position (delaying credit utilization beyond Year 2)
2. Discount rate exceeds 8.5% (breakeven: 120.0 / 110.4 - 1)
3. Credit AMT limitations reduce direct utilization value
4. PetroCorp prioritizes balance sheet deleveraging over NPV maximization

**Recommendation:** Direct utilization preferable given PetroCorp's strong tax position, but transfer provides valuable optionality for future years if tax profile changes.

## Worked Example 2: Transfer Pricing for Shared CCUS Infrastructure

**Scenario:**
EuroOil NV (Netherlands) and NordicPetro ASA (Norway) plan a joint CCUS project:

- **Capture facilities**: Both companies build separate capture units at their respective refineries
- **Shared infrastructure**: Common 450 km offshore pipeline (NL to Norwegian North Sea) and shared geological storage facility
- **Infrastructure ownership**: Special purpose vehicle "North Sea Carbon Storage BV" (NSCS), owned 60% EuroOil, 40% NordicPetro
- **Infrastructure cost**: EUR 800 million (pipeline: EUR 500 million, storage: EUR 300 million)
- **Operating period**: 20 years
- **Projected volumes**:
  - EuroOil: 3.0 million tonnes CO₂/year
  - NordicPetro: 2.0 million tonnes CO₂/year
  - Total: 5.0 million tonnes CO₂/year

**Tax Considerations:**
- Netherlands corporate tax rate: 25.8%
- Norway petroleum tax rate: 78% (56% special tax + 22% ordinary)
- Netherlands-Norway tax treaty: Permanent establishment rules apply
- Both jurisdictions require arm's-length pricing per OECD Guidelines

**Required:**
1. Evaluate alternative transfer pricing methods for infrastructure cost allocation
2. Determine arm's-length service fees for storage services
3. Analyze tax implications under alternative ownership structures
4. Calculate effective tax rates and recommend optimal structure

### Solution Part 1: Transfer Pricing Method Selection

**Alternative Methods Analysis:**

**Method A: Cost Contribution Arrangement (CCA)**

Participants share costs based on expected benefit allocation:

| Participant | CO₂ Volume (Mtpa) | Percentage | Infrastructure Share (EUR M) | Annual Share (EUR M) |
|-------------|------------------|------------|----------------------------|---------------------|
| EuroOil | 3.0 | 60% | 480 | 24.0 |
| NordicPetro | 2.0 | 40% | 320 | 16.0 |
| **Total** | **5.0** | **100%** | **800** | **40.0** |

**Calculation:** EUR 800M / 20 years = EUR 40M annual amortization

**CCA Characteristics:**
- **Benefits**: Aligned with OECD TPG Chapter VIII; each party obtains proportional share of capacity rights
- **Risks**: Requires contemporaneous documentation of participant contributions and expected benefits
- **Balancing payments**: If actual volumes deviate from 60:40 split, true-up payments required

**Method B: Tolling/Service Fee Arrangement**

NSCS charges each user a fee per tonne of CO₂ stored:

**Cost Buildup Analysis:**

| Cost Component | Annual Cost (EUR M) | Unit Cost (EUR/tonne) |
|----------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Depreciation (EUR 800M / 20y) | 40.0 | 8.00 |
| Operating costs (estimate) | 15.0 | 3.00 |
| Insurance and monitoring | 5.0 | 1.00 |
| **Total Cost** | **60.0** | **12.00** |
| Markup (15% cost-plus) | 9.0 | 1.80 |
| **Arm's-Length Fee** | **69.0** | **13.80** |

**Fee charged to each user:** EUR 13.80/tonne

**Benchmarking Analysis:**
Limited public comparables for CCUS transport/storage, but analogous services:

| Comparable | Service | Geography | Fee Range (EUR/tonne CO₂) |
|------------|---------|-----------|--------------------------|
| Porthos Project | Rotterdam CCUS | Netherlands | EUR 30-40 (all-in) |
| Northern Lights | Norway storage | Norway | EUR 80-100 (capture + storage) |
| Aramis Project | Dutch offshore | Netherlands | Not disclosed |

**Analysis:** EUR 13.80/tonne for transport + storage falls within lower quartile of observed pricing, appropriate given:
- Shared infrastructure reduces per-unit costs
- Related-party arrangement eliminates commercial margin
- 15% cost-plus markup reflects low-risk service provision (no commodity price or volume risk)

**Method C: Buy-In to Existing Infrastructure**

If NordicPetro joins project after EuroOil has funded development:

**Buy-in payment calculation:**

EuroOil incurs EUR 800M development costs during Years 1-2. NordicPetro joins in Year 3, acquiring 40% interest.

| Item | Calculation | Amount (EUR M) |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| Historical cost | 40% × EUR 800M | 320.0 |
| Return on invested capital | 40% × EUR 800M × [(1.08)^2 - 1] | 53.2 |
| Development risk premium (10%) | 40% × EUR 800M × 10% | 32.0 |
| **Total Buy-In Payment** | | **405.2** |

**OECD TPG Guidance:** Buy-in payment compensates for time-value of money (8% discount rate) and development risk borne solely by EuroOil.

### Solution Part 2: Recommended Structure and Tax Analysis

**Recommended Approach: Cost Contribution Arrangement**

**Rationale:**
1. **Economic substance**: Reflects joint development and proportional benefit sharing
2. **Tax efficiency**: Avoids profit element in cross-border payments (reduces withholding tax exposure)
3. **Simplicity**: No ongoing fee negotiations or benchmark updates required
4. **Audit defensibility**: Strong OECD TPG Chapter VIII support

**Tax Implications Under CCA:**

**Netherlands Tax Treatment (EuroOil):**

| Year | Capital Contribution | Depreciation (10% SL) | Tax Benefit @ 25.8% | Cash Flow |
|------|---------------------|----------------------|---------------------|-----------|
| 1-2 | (240) | - | - | (240) |
| 3-22 | - | 24 | 6.2 | 6.2 |

**Norway Tax Treatment (NordicPetro):**

| Year | Capital Contribution | Depreciation (100% + Uplift) | Tax Benefit @ 78% | Cash Flow |
|------|---------------------|----------------------------|-------------------|-----------|
| 1-2 | (160) | - | - | (160) |
| 3 | - | 198.4 (160 × 1.24) | 154.8 | 154.8 |
| 4-22 | - | Uplift remaining | ~8.0/year | ~8.0/year |

**Notes:**
- Netherlands: 10-year straight-line depreciation typical for infrastructure
- Norway: Immediate expensing + 24% uplift (5.2% per year for 4 years) significantly enhances economics
- Norway effective subsidy: 78% × 24% = 18.7% of qualifying costs

**Effective After-Tax Cost Comparison:**

| Participant | Nominal Cost | Tax Benefit (NPV @ 8%) | After-Tax Cost | Effective Rate |
|-------------|--------------|----------------------|----------------|----------------|
| EuroOil (NL) | EUR 480M | EUR 94M | EUR 386M | 19.6% |
| NordicPetro (NO) | EUR 320M | EUR 155M | EUR 165M | 48.4% |

**Key Insight:** Norwegian tax regime's immediate expensing and uplift dramatically reduce after-tax project cost, subsidizing 48.4% of nominal investment. This asymmetry may justify NordicPetro accepting smaller ownership percentage despite equal bargaining power.

### Solution Part 3: Permanent Establishment Analysis

**Storage Facility in Norwegian Waters:**

**OECD Model Article 5(1) – Fixed Place of Business:**
- **Fixed place**: Geological storage reservoir constitutes fixed place in Norwegian territory
- **At disposal**: NSCS has legal right to use storage formation under Norwegian license
- **Business activity**: Core activity of CO₂ storage conducted through facility

**Conclusion:** Norwegian PE exists for NSCS BV

**Article 5(4) Preparatory/Auxiliary Exception:**
Does NOT apply because:
- Storage is not preparatory to main business; it IS the main business
- Exception intended for supporting activities (information gathering, purchasing), not principal revenue-generating operations

**Tax Implications:**

| Entity | PE Jurisdiction | Taxable Income Attribution | Tax Rate |
|--------|----------------|---------------------------|----------|
| NSCS BV | Norway | 100% of storage profits | 78% |
| NSCS BV | Netherlands | Nil (treaty exemption) | - |

**Income Attribution (AOA – Authorized OECD Approach):**

Under OECD PE profit attribution rules:
- **Functionally separate enterprise**: Treat Norwegian PE as if it were separate entity
- **Pricing**: Charge Dutch head office arm's-length fee for CO₂ delivered to storage site
- **Capital allocation**: Attribute EUR 300M storage costs to PE (vs. EUR 500M pipeline costs to Dutch head office)

**Simplified Illustration (Year 10 operations):**

| Item | Norwegian PE | Dutch Head Office |
|------|--------------|-------------------|
| Revenue (storage fees) | EUR 30M (5Mt × EUR 6/t) | EUR 39M (5Mt × EUR 7.80/t transport) |
| Operating costs | (5M) | (10M) |
| Depreciation | (15M) | (25M) |
| **Taxable Income** | **EUR 10M** | **EUR 4M** |
| Tax rate | 78% | 25.8% |
| **Tax liability** | **EUR 7.8M** | **EUR 1.0M** |

**Total Tax:** EUR 8.8M on EUR 14M global income = 62.9% effective rate

**Foreign Tax Credit (Netherlands):**
- **Creditable tax**: EUR 7.8M Norwegian tax on PE income
- **FTC limitation**: Lesser of (a) foreign tax paid or (b) NL tax on foreign income
- **Calculation**: EUR 10M × 25.8% = EUR 2.58M limitation
- **Excess foreign tax**: EUR 5.22M (not creditable)

**Key Challenge:** Norwegian 78% rate vastly exceeds Dutch 25.8% rate, creating excess FTC that provides no benefit. This favors operating storage via Norwegian subsidiary (vs. PE) to obtain participation exemption treatment.

### Solution Part 4: Alternative Structure Recommendation

**Optimized Structure: Norwegian Storage Subsidiary**

Replace NSCS BV with:
1. **NSCS Transport BV** (Netherlands): Owns pipeline, charges EUR 7.80/tonne transport fee
2. **NSCS Storage AS** (Norway): Owns geological storage, charges EUR 6.00/tonne storage fee

**Tax Treatment:**

**Dutch Parent (EuroOil perspective):**
- **Dividend from NSCS Storage AS**: Exempt under NL participation exemption (>5% shareholding in active subsidiary)
- **Effective rate**: Only 25.8% on Dutch-source transport income
- **No excess FTC**: Norwegian income not included in Dutch tax base

**Consolidated Effective Rate (EuroOil Group):**

| Income Source | Taxable Income | Tax Rate | Tax Liability |
|---------------|----------------|----------|---------------|
| Dutch transport | EUR 4M | 25.8% | EUR 1.0M |
| Norwegian storage | EUR 10M | 78% | EUR 7.8M |
| **Total** | **EUR 14M** | **62.9%** | **EUR 8.8M** |

**Result:** Same total tax, but participation exemption avoids excess FTC issue, preserving value of future Dutch losses or credits.
