# Technical Services Background - Energy Resources

## 1. Core Framework

### 1.1 Definition of Technical Services in Energy Sector

**Technical services** in the energy sector comprise specialized knowledge-based services that support exploration, development, production, and processing of hydrocarbon resources. Unlike operating contracts (concessions, PSCs) where parties share production, technical services are compensated through **fee-based remuneration** for expertise, technology, and know-how provided.

**Distinguishing Characteristics**:
- **Knowledge transfer**: Provision of specialized technical expertise, not capital or physical resources
- **Fee-based compensation**: Fixed fees, daily rates, cost-plus arrangements (not production sharing)
- **No resource ownership**: Service provider has no interest in underlying hydrocarbons
- **Expertise-driven**: Relies on human capital, technical know-how, proprietary methodologies
- **Cross-border nature**: Often provided by non-resident entities to host country operators

### 1.2 Types of Technical Services in Energy Sector

The energy sector encompasses diverse technical services across the value chain:

**1. Geological and Geophysical Services**:
- **Seismic surveys**: 2D/3D seismic data acquisition and processing
- **Geological studies**: Basin analysis, stratigraphic studies, geological modeling
- **Geophysical interpretation**: Seismic interpretation, structural analysis
- **Gravity and magnetic surveys**: Regional exploration data
- **Remote sensing**: Satellite imagery analysis for surface geology

**2. Drilling and Well Services**:
- **Drilling supervision**: Wellsite geology, drilling engineering oversight
- **Well planning**: Trajectory design, casing programs, drilling fluid design
- **Formation evaluation**: Well logging, mud logging, core analysis
- **Well testing**: Drill stem testing, production testing, pressure transient analysis
- **Completion design**: Perforation strategy, artificial lift selection

**3. Engineering and Technical Consultancy**:
- **Reservoir engineering**: Reservoir simulation, decline curve analysis, recovery optimization
- **Production engineering**: Artificial lift design, well performance optimization, flow assurance
- **Process engineering**: Facilities design, process optimization, debottlenecking studies
- **Pipeline engineering**: Pipeline design, integrity assessment, flow modeling
- **Project management**: FEED studies, EPC management, commissioning support

**4. Asset Management and Secondments**:
- **Seconded personnel**: Expatriate technical staff placed with operator
- **Integrated operations**: Real-time operations centers, digital oilfield services
- **Maintenance management**: Asset integrity, turnaround planning
- **Operations support**: Production optimization, field development planning
- **HSE consultancy**: Safety management systems, environmental compliance

**5. Specialized Technical Services**:
- **Laboratory analysis**: Fluid analysis (PVT), geochemistry, petrophysics
- **Well intervention**: Workover design, stimulation engineering
- **Enhanced oil recovery (EOR)**: Waterflooding design, chemical EOR, thermal EOR
- **Decommissioning**: Abandonment planning, plugging and abandonment (P&A) engineering
- **Digital services**: Data analytics, machine learning applications, digital twins

### 1.3 Distinction from Other Services

**Technical Services vs. Construction/Installation Services**:
- **Technical services**: Knowledge-based, advice, studies, engineering design
- **Construction services**: Physical execution, installation, building activities
- **Tax treatment**: Construction creates PE under Article 5(3); technical services may have separate treaty article

**Technical Services vs. Management Services**:
- **Technical services**: Specialized technical expertise in oil and gas operations
- **Management services**: Administrative, organizational, general business management
- **Treaty treatment**: Management services typically covered by Article 7 (Business Profits)

**Technical Services vs. Royalties**:
- **Technical services**: Active provision of services, continuous involvement
- **Royalties**: Passive use of intellectual property (patents, know-how, copyrights)
- **Critical distinction**: Significant tax implications (Article 7 vs. Article 12)

**Independent vs. Dependent Personal Services**:
- **Independent**: Service provider retains control, bears risk, uses own resources
- **Dependent**: Seconded personnel under client control (employment relationship)
- **Historical note**: Article 14 (Independent Personal Services) deleted from OECD Model (2000); now covered under Article 7

## 2. Technical Rules

### 2.1 Domestic Law Treatment of Technical Services

**Source Country Taxation Rights**:

Most petroleum-producing countries assert **source taxation** of technical services income through:

**1. Corporate Income Tax**:
- Tax on profits if service provider has taxable presence (branch, PE, subsidiary)
- Requires filing tax return, maintaining books and records
- Tax on net income (revenue minus allowable expenses)

**2. Withholding Tax (WHT)**:
- Applied to gross service fees paid to non-resident service providers
- Rates vary: 10-30% of gross payments
- Final or creditable tax depending on domestic law
- Administrative convenience: Client withholds and remits tax

**3. Specific Technical Services Tax Regimes**:
- **India**: Equalization levy (2% on e-commerce services); Fees for Technical Services (FTS) provisions
- **Nigeria**: WHT at 10% on technical services; higher rates (20-30%) for some services
- **Indonesia**: WHT at 20% on technical services (treaty may reduce)
- **Brazil**: Services tax (ISS) at municipal level plus WHT on cross-border payments

**Domestic Law WHT Rates (Examples)**:

| **Country** | **Technical Services WHT Rate** | **Statutory Reference** |
|------------|-------------------------------|------------------------|
| **India** | 10% (plus surcharge/cess) | Section 115A, 206AA Income Tax Act |
| **Nigeria** | 10% | Section 78, Finance Act 2020 |
| **Indonesia** | 20% (reduced for treaty) | Article 26, Income Tax Law |
| **Brazil** | 15-25% (IRRF) | Law 9,779/1999 |
| **Kazakhstan** | 20% | Article 645, Tax Code |
| **Angola** | 6.5% | Industrial Tax Code |
| **Mexico** | 25% (some technical services) | Article 200, Income Tax Law |

### 2.2 Treaty Treatment - Article 7 vs. Article 14 vs. Technical Services Article

**Article 7 (Business Profits) - OECD Model Default**:
- **Scope**: Covers all business income not specifically addressed in other articles
- **Taxation right**: Exclusive to residence country **unless** business carried on through PE in source country
- **PE requirement**: Source country can tax only if PE exists; tax limited to profits attributable to PE
- **Technical services**: Covered under Article 7 unless treaty includes specific technical services article

**Article 14 (Independent Personal Services) - Historical**:
- **Status**: Deleted from OECD Model (2000); still exists in older treaties
- **Scope**: Professional services (physicians, lawyers, engineers, architects, dentists, accountants)
- **Fixed base concept**: Similar to PE but for professional services
- **Threshold**: Remuneration taxable if:
  - Professional has fixed base in source country, OR
  - Stays in source country ≥183 days in 12-month period
- **Modern treatment**: Merged into Article 7 (no functional difference from PE concept)

**Technical Services Article (UN Model Article 12A) - Emerging Provision**:
- **Purpose**: Grants source country taxing rights on fees for technical services even without PE
- **Not in OECD Model**: Reflects developing country concerns about base erosion
- **Included in**: UN Model (2017 addition), many bilateral treaties with developing countries
- **Mechanism**: WHT on gross fees or net taxation if PE exists

### 2.3 Treaty Comparison - Article 7 vs. Technical Services Article

**Comparative Analysis**:

| **Aspect** | **Article 7 (OECD Model)** | **Technical Services Article** |
|-----------|--------------------------|------------------------------|
| **PE requirement** | Yes - requires PE for source taxation | No - source country can tax without PE |
| **Tax base** | Net profits (expenses deductible) | Gross fees (WHT) or net if elected |
| **Tax rate** | Corporate tax rate (20-35%) | Treaty WHT rate (5-15% typical) |
| **Compliance** | File tax return, transfer pricing docs | Simple WHT deduction by payer |
| **Definition** | Broad - all business income | Specific - technical services defined |
| **Services scope** | All business activities | Technical, managerial, consultancy |
| **Make-available** | Not applicable | Often includes make-available condition |

**Example Treaty Provisions**:

**India-Singapore DTA (2005)**:
- Article 12(4): Fees for technical services (FTS) may be taxed in source country
- Rate: 10% of gross amount (reduced from 15% under domestic law)
- Definition: Includes managerial, technical, or consultancy services
- Make-available clause: **No** (source taxation regardless of knowledge transfer)

**China-Pakistan DTA**:
- Article 12A: Technical services taxable in source country at 10%
- Scope: Technical or consultancy services (excluding Article 14 services)
- Make-available: **Yes** (tax only if services make technology available to recipient)

### 2.4 Withholding Tax Implications

**WHT Mechanism**:
1. **Obligor**: Service recipient (operator) required to withhold from gross payment
2. **Rate**: Domestic law rate or reduced treaty rate (if applicable)
3. **Timing**: Withheld at time of payment or credit (whichever earlier)
4. **Remittance**: Withheld tax remitted to tax authority (monthly/quarterly)
5. **Credit**: Service provider may claim foreign tax credit in residence country

**Treaty Relief Procedures**:
- **Certificate of Residence (COR)**: Obtained from service provider's tax authority
- **Application for reduced rate**: Submitted to source country tax authority before payment
- **Clearance certificate**: Pre-approval of reduced WHT rate
- **Refund claim**: Pay full WHT, claim refund later (less common due to delays)

**Common WHT Issues**:
- **Gross-up clauses**: Contract specifies net payment; payer bears WHT cost
  - Tax calculation: Gross payment = Net / (1 - WHT rate)
  - Example: $100k net, 10% WHT → Gross = $100k / 0.90 = $111,111; WHT = $11,111
- **Multiple services**: Distinguishing technical services from royalties, management fees
- **Related party transactions**: Transfer pricing scrutiny of service fees
- **Treaty shopping**: Routing through low-WHT treaty jurisdictions

### 2.5 PE Creation Risks for Technical Services

**Technical services may create PE through multiple pathways**:

**1. Fixed Place of Business PE (Article 5(1))**:
- **Office/facility**: Permanent office for project management, technical support
- **Project site**: Extended presence at operator's site (if premises at service provider's disposal)
- **Equipment depot**: Storage and maintenance facility for technical equipment
- **Threshold**: No specific time limit; permanence and regularity assessed

**2. Service PE (UN Model Article 5(3)(b))**:
- **Provision**: Furnishing of services through employees or personnel
- **Threshold**: 183 days (or lower) within 12-month period for same or connected project
- **Calculation**: Aggregate time of all service provider personnel in source country
- **Not in OECD Model**: Only if bilateral treaty adopts UN Model provision

**3. Construction/Installation PE (Article 5(3))**:
- **Scope**: Technical supervision of construction/installation may create PE
- **Threshold**: Typically 6-12 months depending on treaty
- **Example**: Technical services for offshore platform installation (12+ months)

**4. Dependent Agent PE (Article 5(5))**:
- **Agent**: Local representative with authority to conclude service contracts
- **Habitual exercise**: Regularly negotiates and binds service provider
- **Risk**: Local sales agents, coordinators with contracting authority

**5. Seconded Personnel Issues**:
- **Control test**: Who directs day-to-day work (service provider or client)?
- **Service PE**: If service provider retains control → service PE risk
- **Employment PE**: If client controls personnel → potentially creates client's PE
- **Transfer pricing**: Arm's length remuneration critical (cost-plus vs. full service margin)

### 2.6 Make-Available Provision

**Concept**: Some treaties limit source taxation of technical services to situations where services **make technical knowledge, skills, or know-how available** to the service recipient.

**Purpose**: Distinguish genuine knowledge transfer from routine services where no permanent benefit accrues to recipient.

**Definition** (UN Model Commentary):
- Services enable recipient to apply technology independently in future
- Knowledge transfer is **main purpose** or **important result** of service engagement
- Recipient acquires know-how, not just receives one-time service

**Examples**:

**Make-Available (Taxable)**:
- Training recipient's engineers to operate new technology
- Transfer of proprietary software with source code and documentation
- Geological interpretation methods taught to recipient's geologists
- Reservoir simulation model built and explained to recipient (full knowledge transfer)

**Not Make-Available (Not Taxable under Make-Available Treaties)**:
- Seismic data acquisition service (data provided, but acquisition methodology not transferred)
- Laboratory analysis service (results provided, not analytical techniques)
- Engineering study prepared by consultant (report delivered, consultant retains methodology)
- Drilling supervision (oversight provided, drilling know-how retained by service provider)

**Treaty Examples with Make-Available**:
- **Canada-India DTA**: Article 12(5) includes make-available condition
- **Netherlands-India DTA**: FTS taxable only if make services, skills, or know-how available
- **China-Pakistan DTA**: Technical services taxable if make technology available

**Impact**:
- **Narrow interpretation**: Significant technical services may escape source taxation
- **Compliance complexity**: Determining whether "make-available" test satisfied
- **Documentation**: Contracts should clearly state whether knowledge transfer intended

## 3. Analytical Depth

### 3.1 Services vs. Royalties Distinction

**Critical Tax Significance**: Services taxed under Article 7 (net basis, PE requirement) vs. royalties under Article 12 (gross WHT, no PE requirement).

**OECD Model Definitions**:
- **Royalties (Article 12)**: Payments for use of, or right to use:
  - Industrial, commercial, scientific equipment
  - Patents, trademarks, designs, models, plans
  - Copyrights, software
  - Know-how (secret formulas, processes, information concerning industrial, commercial, scientific experience)
- **Technical services (Article 7)**: Active provision of services using expertise

**Distinction Framework**:

| **Factor** | **Technical Services** | **Royalties** |
|-----------|----------------------|--------------|
| **Nature** | Active provision of services | Passive use of property |
| **Provider involvement** | Continuous, active involvement | Minimal involvement after licensing |
| **Recipient benefit** | Service results, advice, execution | Right to use IP independently |
| **Duration** | Project-based, finite engagement | Ongoing license for period |
| **Risk** | Service provider bears performance risk | IP owner bears no performance risk |
| **Know-how transfer** | Incidental to service | Central to transaction |

**Borderline Cases - Seismic Data**:

**Scenario 1: Seismic Data Acquisition Service**:
- Service provider conducts seismic survey
- Processes data and delivers interpreted data to operator
- **Analysis**: Technical service (Article 7) - active service provision
- **Rationale**: Service provider uses equipment and expertise to gather/process data; not licensing existing know-how

**Scenario 2: Seismic Data Library License**:
- Service provider licenses access to pre-existing seismic data library
- Operator pays license fee to access historical data
- **Analysis**: Royalty (Article 12) - passive use of pre-existing information
- **Rationale**: No new services provided; operator pays for right to use existing data

**Scenario 3: Proprietary Software Use**:
- Operator uses service provider's proprietary reservoir simulation software
- Pay-per-use or license fee model
- **Analysis**: Royalty (Article 12) if copyright/know-how license; service (Article 7) if provider runs simulations
- **Rationale**: Depends on whether operator independently uses software or provider operates software as service

**OECD Guidance** (Commentaries on Article 12):
- Paragraph 11: Payments for services not ordinarily treated as royalties unless include technology transfer
- Borderline cases: Focus on substance - active service vs. passive IP use

**Tax Planning Implications**:
- **Characterization critical**: Service (net taxation with PE) vs. royalty (gross WHT)
- **Contract drafting**: Clearly specify nature of transaction
- **Substance over form**: Tax authorities examine actual activities, not labels

### 3.2 Transfer Pricing for Technical Services

**Arm's Length Principle** (OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines): Related party technical services priced as if between independent parties.

**Transfer Pricing Methods for Technical Services**:

**1. Cost Plus Method (CPM)**:
- **Application**: Service provider compensated for costs plus markup
- **Calculation**: Total costs × (1 + markup percentage)
- **Typical markups**: 5-15% for routine services; 15-30% for specialized services
- **Advantages**: Simple, objective cost base
- **Disadvantages**: Ignores market prices, may not reflect value

**2. Transactional Net Margin Method (TNMM)**:
- **Application**: Compare service provider's net margin to independent comparables
- **PLI (Profit Level Indicator)**: Operating margin (EBIT/Sales) most common
- **Benchmarking**: Identify comparable independent service companies
- **Advantages**: Functional comparability focus
- **Disadvantages**: Finding comparables challenging for specialized energy services

**3. Comparable Uncontrolled Price (CUP)**:
- **Application**: Compare service fees to unrelated party transactions
- **Requirements**: Highly comparable services, similar terms
- **Advantages**: Most direct method if comparables available
- **Disadvantages**: Rarely available for specialized technical services

**4. Profit Split Method**:
- **Application**: Complex, integrated services where each party contributes unique value
- **Allocation**: Split combined profits based on relative contributions
- **Use cases**: Joint technical development, integrated engineering services
- **Advantages**: Appropriate for unique, integrated arrangements
- **Disadvantages**: Complex, requires detailed functional analysis

**Key Transfer Pricing Issues**:

**Shareholder Services**: Services benefiting parent company, not recipient (non-deductible)
- **Incidental benefit test**: Does recipient actually benefit?
- **Duplication**: Are in-house capabilities duplicated by parent services?

**Service Fees vs. Cost Recharges**:
- **Active services**: Markup justified (5-30%)
- **Cost allocation**: Minimal/no markup (0-3%)
- **Example**: Technical personnel secondment (cost-plus 10%) vs. corporate overhead allocation (cost-only)

**Make-or-Buy Analysis**:
- Would recipient obtain services from third party if not provided by related party?
- If no, services may not be arm's length

**Documentation Requirements** (OECD BEPS Actions 13):
- **Master file**: Group structure, transfer pricing policies
- **Local file**: Detailed analysis of related party technical services
- **CbCR**: Country-by-country reporting (if group revenue >€750 million)

### 3.3 Independent vs. Dependent Services

**Distinction Importance**: Affects PE risk and profit attribution.

**Independent Services (Service Provider Controlled)**:
- **Characteristics**:
  - Service provider retains operational control
  - Uses own equipment, technology, personnel
  - Bears performance risk (results, quality, timing)
  - Invoices for services provided
  - Client specifies desired outcome, not methodology
- **Tax treatment**:
  - Service PE risk if personnel present >threshold
  - Full service margin justified (15-30%+ depending on risk and value)
  - Article 7 (Business Profits) applies

**Dependent Services (Client Controlled) / Secondments**:
- **Characteristics**:
  - Client directs day-to-day activities
  - Personnel integrated into client organization
  - Client bears operational risk
  - Personnel work at client premises under client supervision
  - Invoicing on time-and-materials basis
- **Tax treatment**:
  - Lower PE risk for service provider (no fixed place at disposal)
  - Lower markup justified (5-15% cost-plus typical)
  - Substance test: Is it genuine secondment or disguised service PE?

**Control Test Factors**:
1. **Supervision**: Who directs daily work activities?
2. **Equipment**: Provided by service provider or client?
3. **Premises**: Service provider's office or client's facility?
4. **Performance risk**: Who bears risk of unsatisfactory performance?
5. **Results vs. activity**: Client purchases deliverables (independent) or time (dependent)?

**Example**:
- **Independent**: Drilling contractor operates rig under day-rate contract; retains operational control
- **Dependent**: Seconded reservoir engineer works at client's office under client's direction

### 3.4 Cost-Plus vs. Fee-Based Structures

**Remuneration Models for Technical Services**:

**1. Cost-Plus (Cost Reimbursement Plus Margin)**:
- **Structure**: Reimbursement of actual costs + agreed markup percentage
- **Costs**: Direct personnel, travel, equipment, subcontractors
- **Markup**: 5-30% depending on service complexity and risk
- **Advantages**:
  - Low client risk (pays actual costs)
  - Appropriate for uncertain scope
  - Common for secondments, FEED studies
- **Disadvantages**:
  - No cost discipline for service provider
  - Open-ended client commitment
  - Transfer pricing scrutiny (related parties)

**Typical Cost-Plus Markups** (Arm's Length):
- Routine administrative services: 3-8%
- Technical personnel secondment: 5-15%
- Engineering studies: 10-20%
- Complex integrated services: 15-30%

**2. Lump-Sum (Fixed Fee)**:
- **Structure**: Fixed price for defined scope
- **Risk allocation**: Service provider bears cost overrun risk
- **Advantages**:
  - Client cost certainty
  - Incentive for provider efficiency
  - Clean transfer pricing (market-based)
- **Disadvantages**:
  - Requires well-defined scope
  - Scope change disputes
  - Higher price to cover provider risk

**3. Day-Rate / Time-and-Materials**:
- **Structure**: Daily rates for personnel/equipment + materials at cost
- **Application**: Drilling rigs, well services, staff augmentation
- **Risk**: Client bears quantity risk; provider bears rate risk
- **Advantages**: Flexible for variable duration
- **Disadvantages**: Open-ended cost, rate negotiation complexity

**4. Performance-Based / Incentive Fees**:
- **Structure**: Base fee + performance bonus (KPIs achieved)
- **Application**: Production optimization, EOR projects
- **KPIs**: Production increase, recovery factor improvement, cost reduction
- **Advantages**: Aligns interests, rewards value creation
- **Disadvantages**: Complex metrics, measurement disputes

**Transfer Pricing Considerations**:
- **Related parties**: Cost-plus requires detailed cost substantiation
- **Independent parties**: Lump-sum or day-rate more common (market negotiation)
- **Profit level**: Must be arm's length relative to functions, risks, assets

## 4. Application Methodology

### 4.1 Technical Services Classification Framework

**Step-by-Step Classification Process**:

**Step 1: Identify Nature of Transaction**
- Is payment for services (active provider involvement) or property use (passive IP license)?
- If service → proceed to Step 2
- If royalty → Article 12 applies (separate analysis)

**Step 2: Determine Service Type**
- Construction/installation services → Article 5(3) construction PE
- Technical/consultancy services → Article 7 or technical services article (if in treaty)
- Management services → Article 7 (business profits)
- Professional services (engineer, architect) → Article 7 (or Article 14 if old treaty)

**Step 3: Check Treaty Provisions**
- Does treaty include specific technical services article?
  - **Yes** → Source country WHT right (typically 5-15%) without PE requirement
  - **No** → Article 7 applies (source taxation only if PE)
- Does technical services article include make-available provision?
  - **Yes** → Assess whether knowledge transfer occurs
  - **No** → All technical services potentially taxable at source

**Step 4: Assess PE Risk**
- **Fixed place**: Office, project site, equipment depot?
- **Service PE**: Personnel presence > treaty threshold (if UN Model adopted)?
- **Construction PE**: Construction/installation supervision > threshold?
- **Dependent agent**: Local representative with contracting authority?

**Step 5: Determine Tax Treatment**
- **No PE + no technical services article**: Tax-exempt in source country (Article 7)
- **No PE + technical services article**: WHT at treaty rate on gross fees
- **PE exists**: Source country taxes profits attributable to PE (net basis)

### 4.2 Tax Treatment Decision Tree

**Decision Tree for Technical Services Taxation**:

```
[Technical Services Income]
        |
        ├─ Q1: Is treaty in place?
        |   ├─ NO → Domestic law applies
        |   |        ├─ WHT on gross (10-30% typical)
        |   |        └─ PE taxation if domestic PE exists
        |   |
        |   └─ YES → Proceed to Q2
        |
        ├─ Q2: Does treaty include Technical Services Article?
        |   ├─ YES → Proceed to Q3
        |   |
        |   └─ NO → Article 7 applies
        |            ├─ Q5: PE exists?
        |            |   ├─ YES → Tax profits attributable to PE
        |            |   └─ NO → Source country cannot tax
        |
        ├─ Q3: Make-available provision in treaty?
        |   ├─ YES → Proceed to Q4
        |   |
        |   └─ NO → Source WHT applies at treaty rate
        |            (e.g., 10-15% of gross)
        |
        ├─ Q4: Do services make know-how available?
        |   ├─ YES → Source WHT applies at treaty rate
        |   |
        |   └─ NO → Article 7 applies
        |            └─ Return to Q5 (PE test)
        |
        └─ Q5: Does PE exist in source country?
            ├─ YES → Source country taxes profits attributable to PE
            |        ├─ Net basis (expenses deductible)
            |        ├─ CIT rate (20-35% typical)
            |        └─ Transfer pricing documentation required
            |
            └─ NO → Source country cannot tax
                     (Exclusive residence country taxation)
```

**Key Decision Factors**:
1. **Treaty network**: Which treaty applies based on service provider residence?
2. **Technical services article**: Specific provision or default Article 7?
3. **Make-available clause**: Knowledge transfer requirement or not?
4. **PE thresholds**: Time limits, connected projects, agent relationships
5. **Economic analysis**: WHT on gross vs. CIT on net (which more favorable?)

## 5. Worked Examples

### 5.1 Example 1: Seismic Data Services - Royalty vs. Service Fee Analysis with WHT Comparison

**Facts**:
- **SeismicTech Ltd**: UK-incorporated geophysical services company
- **Client**: KazakhOil (Kazakhstan national oil company)
- **Services**: Two separate transactions:

  **Transaction A - New Seismic Survey**:
  - Conduct 3D seismic survey in offshore Caspian Sea (Kazakhstan sector)
  - Acquire, process, and interpret seismic data
  - Deliver processed seismic volume and interpretation report
  - Duration: 6 months (March-August 2024)
  - Fee: $8 million
  - Costs: $6 million (vessel, crew, processing, interpretation)

  **Transaction B - Seismic Data Library Access**:
  - License access to SeismicTech's existing regional seismic database (1990-2020)
  - KazakhOil obtains non-exclusive rights to use historical seismic data
  - No new data acquisition; purely access to existing database
  - Duration: 3-year license
  - Fee: $2 million (upfront payment)
  - Costs: Negligible (data already exists)

- **Treaty**: UK-Kazakhstan Tax Treaty
  - Article 7: Business Profits (source taxation only if PE)
  - Article 12: Royalties - 10% WHT
  - No specific technical services article
  - No service PE provision (OECD Model)
  - Article 5(3): Construction PE at 12 months

**Issue 1: Characterization - Services vs. Royalties**

**Transaction A - New Seismic Survey Analysis**:

**Royalty Argument** (Kazakhstan Tax Authority):
- Payment includes right to use seismic data (information)
- Article 12(3): Know-how royalties include payments for information concerning industrial, commercial, or scientific experience
- Seismic data constitutes scientific information
- SeismicTech retains intellectual property in survey methodologies

**Service Argument** (SeismicTech):
- Active provision of survey services (vessel, crew, equipment)
- Seismic data is result of service, not license of pre-existing property
- Payment is for execution of work, not passive use of property
- OECD Model Commentary: Payments for services not ordinarily treated as royalties

**Analysis**:
- **OECD Approach**: Technical services (Article 7)
  - SeismicTech actively acquires and processes new data
  - Not licensing pre-existing information
  - Know-how royalty requires transfer of secret formulas, processes, or information with independent economic value
  - Raw seismic data (post-processing) is survey result, not know-how
- **Conclusion**: **Technical service under Article 7**

**Transaction B - Seismic Data Library License Analysis**:

**Characteristics**:
- Passive license of pre-existing database
- No active service provision by SeismicTech
- KazakhOil obtains right to use information independently
- Historical data with independent economic value

**Analysis**:
- **Royalty treatment**: Payment for right to use information
  - Article 12 (Royalties) applies
  - Information concerning scientific experience (seismic database)
  - Passive nature: No ongoing SeismicTech involvement
- **Conclusion**: **Royalty under Article 12**

**Issue 2: PE Analysis for Transaction A**

**Fixed Place PE (Article 5(1))**:
- Seismic vessel operates in Kazakhstan territorial waters/continental shelf
- Duration: 6 months (permanent enough for fixed place)
- **However**: Mobile vessel; no fixed geographical location
- **Conclusion**: Unlikely to constitute fixed place PE (lacks geographical permanence)

**Construction PE (Article 5(3))**:
- Treaty threshold: 12 months
- Survey duration: 6 months
- **Conclusion**: **No construction PE** (below 12-month threshold)

**Service PE**:
- Not applicable - UK-Kazakhstan treaty follows OECD Model (no service PE provision)

**Overall PE Conclusion**: **No PE exists**

**Issue 3: Tax Treatment and WHT Comparison**

**Transaction A Tax Treatment**:

**Scenario 1 - Treated as Service (Correct Treatment)**:
- Article 7 applies
- No PE exists
- **Result**: Kazakhstan cannot tax; **UK exclusive taxation**
- UK tax (assuming 19% rate): $2 million profit × 19% = **$380,000**
- Kazakhstan tax: **$0**
- **Total tax: $380,000**

**Scenario 2 - If Mischaracterized as Royalty**:
- Article 12 applies
- WHT: 10% of gross fee
- **Kazakhstan WHT: $8,000,000 × 10% = $800,000**
- UK tax on profit: $2 million × 19% = $380,000
- UK foreign tax credit: ($380,000) [credit limited to UK tax on foreign income]
- Excess foreign tax: $420,000 (loss - cannot use immediately)
- **Total tax: $800,000** (effectively)

**Comparison**:
- **Service treatment**: $380,000 total tax (UK only)
- **Royalty treatment**: $800,000 total tax (Kazakhstan WHT + UK residual)
- **Difference**: $420,000 (110% higher tax if mischaracterized)

**Transaction B Tax Treatment**:

**Correct Treatment - Royalty**:
- Article 12 applies: 10% WHT
- **Kazakhstan WHT: $2,000,000 × 10% = $200,000**
- SeismicTech profit: ~$2 million (minimal costs)
- UK tax: $2 million × 19% = $380,000
- UK foreign tax credit: ($200,000)
- **Net UK tax: $180,000**
- **Total tax: $380,000** (Kazakhstan $200k + UK $180k)

**Combined Tax Summary**:

| **Transaction** | **Characterization** | **Kazakhstan Tax** | **UK Tax** | **Total Tax** | **Effective Rate** |
|----------------|---------------------|-------------------|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| **A (Survey)** | Service (correct) | $0 | $380,000 | $380,000 | 19.0% (on profit) |
| **A (Survey)** | Royalty (incorrect) | $800,000 | $0 | $800,000 | 10.0% (on gross) |

**Key Takeaways**:
1. **Characterization critical**: Service vs. royalty distinction has major tax impact ($420k difference for Transaction A)
2. **Substance over form**: Active survey service vs. passive data license
3. **Treaty provision**: No PE required for royalty WHT; PE required for service taxation

### 5.2 Example 2: Engineering Consultancy - PE vs. WHT Analysis with Profit Comparison

**Facts**:
- **EngineerCo Pte Ltd**: Singapore-incorporated engineering consultancy firm
- **Services**: Reservoir engineering services for Nigerian operator (NigerianOil Ltd)
- **Project**: Reservoir simulation study and field development plan for offshore oil field
- **Structure**:
  - 3 senior reservoir engineers deployed to Lagos, Nigeria
  - Work from NigerianOil's office (desk space provided)
  - Duration: 10 months (January-October 2024)
  - Monthly visits by Singapore-based technical director (5 visits, 3 days each = 15 days)
  - Reservoir simulation software operated from Singapore (cloud-based)
  - Deliverables: Reservoir model, development scenarios, economic analysis, FDP report
- **Commercial Terms**:
  - Contract value: $1.5 million
  - Breakdown:
    - Personnel: $1.2 million (3 engineers × $400k each)
    - Software/tools: $100,000
    - Singapore technical support: $200,000
  - Costs:
    - Engineer salaries/benefits: $900,000 (loaded cost)
    - Singapore overhead (director, facilities, admin): $300,000
    - Software licenses: $50,000
    - Total costs: $1,250,000
  - **Profit margin: 16.7%** ($250,000 profit)

- **Treaty**: Singapore-Nigeria Tax Treaty
  - Article 5(3)(b): **Service PE provision**
    - Services through employees/personnel for >**90 days** within any 12-month period for same or connected project
  - Article 7: Business Profits (taxable only if PE)
  - Article 12: Royalties - 7.5% WHT
  - **No specific technical services article**

**Issue 1: Does EngineerCo have a Service PE in Nigeria?**

**Service PE Analysis (Article 5(3)(b))**:

**Threshold Test**:
- **Personnel presence**:
  - 3 engineers: 10 months each = 300 days each
  - Technical director: 15 days
- **Aggregation method** (Commentary): Days personnel present, not man-days
  - Alternative view: Aggregate individual presence days
  - **Conservative approach**: Count each engineer separately, then determine if threshold exceeded

**Calculation**:

| **Personnel** | **Period in Nigeria** | **Days** | **Threshold (90 days)** |
|--------------|---------------------|---------|------------------------|
| Engineer 1 | Jan-Oct 2024 (continuous) | 304 days | **Exceeded** |
| Engineer 2 | Jan-Oct 2024 (continuous) | 304 days | **Exceeded** |
| Engineer 3 | Jan-Oct 2024 (continuous) | 304 days | **Exceeded** |
| Technical Director | 5 visits × 3 days | 15 days | Not exceeded |

**Conclusion**: **Service PE exists** - multiple personnel present for >90 days for same project (reservoir study).

**Fixed Place PE Analysis**:
- **Premises**: Engineers work at client's office (not EngineerCo premises)
- **Disposal test**: Desk space provided by client; not "at disposal" of EngineerCo
- **Conclusion**: **No fixed place PE** (OECD Commentary: Personnel working at client site under client control generally don't create fixed place PE)

**Dependent Agent PE**:
- No local agent with contracting authority
- Not applicable

**Overall PE Conclusion**: **Service PE exists under Article 5(3)(b)**

**Issue 2: Profit Attribution to Nigerian PE**

**OECD Authorized Approach (AOA)**:

**Step 1 - Functional Analysis**:
- **PE functions**:

**Step 2 - Asset Attribution**:

- **Head office assets**: Reservoir simulation software, technical library, corporate IP

**Step 3 - Risk Attribution**:
- **Performance risk**: Quality and accuracy of reservoir study - allocated to PE (where work performed)
- **Client credit risk**: Retained by head office (contractual relationship)
- **Key personnel functions**: Engineers in Nigeria bear primary execution risk

**Step 4 - Profit Attribution**:

**Revenue Attribution**:
- Total revenue: $1,500,000
- **PE revenue**: $1,500,000 (all revenue from Nigerian project)

  - Administrative support (HR, finance, legal): $170,000

**Issue 3: Nigerian Tax Calculation**

**Scenario A: PE Taxation (Actual)**

- Taxable profit: $430,000

**Total Tax (PE Scenario)**:

- Singapore tax: $0
- **Total: $129,000**

## 6. Recent Developments (2023-2025)

### 6.1 UN Model Addition of Article 12A (Technical Services)

**2017 UN Model Update**: Added Article 12A specifically addressing Fees for Technical Services (FTS).

**Purpose**: Provide developing countries explicit taxing rights over technical services income without requiring PE.

**Article 12A Key Features**:
- **Definition**: Payments for managerial, technical, or consultancy services
- **Source taxation**: Source country may tax at negotiated treaty rate (typically 7.5-15%)
- **Exclusions**: Payments for services that make up part of the profits of an independent agent
- **Optional make-available clause**: Parties may include condition that services make technical knowledge available

**Adoption Status**:
- **Not in OECD Model**: OECD maintains Article 7 approach (PE requirement for source taxation)
- **Bilateral adoption**: Increasing number of treaties with developing countries include technical services provisions
- **Examples**:
  - India's tax treaties: Extensive FTS provisions (pre-dating Article 12A formalization)
  - African countries: Kenya, Uganda, Zambia treaties include technical services articles
  - Latin America: Brazil, Argentina treaties with some countries include FTS provisions

**Trend**: Growing acceptance of source taxation for technical services in treaties involving developing economies.

### 6.2 BEPS 2.0 and Pillar Two Impact

**Pillar Two (Global Minimum Tax - 15%)**: Effective from 2024-2025 for MNE groups with revenue >€750 million.

**Impact on Technical Services Companies**:
- **Larger service companies**: Subject to 15% minimum tax calculation
- **Jurisdictional blending**: Profits and taxes calculated on per-jurisdiction basis
- **Top-up tax**: If effective tax rate in jurisdiction <15%, top-up tax imposed
- **PE allocation**: Pillar Two rules allocate profits to PEs for jurisdictional calculation

**Example**:
- EngineerCo group revenue: €1 billion (Pillar Two applies)
- Nigerian PE: Effective tax rate 30% (above 15% minimum) → No top-up tax
- Singapore head office: Effective tax rate 12% (below 15%) → Top-up tax to reach 15%
- **Result**: Singapore top-up tax = 3% of Singapore profits

**Practical Effect**: Reduces benefit of routing technical services through low-tax jurisdictions (UAE, Singapore if rate <15%).

### 6.3 Digitalization and Remote Service Delivery

**COVID-19 Acceleration**: Pandemic normalized remote technical services (digital oilfield, remote monitoring, virtual consultancy).

**PE Implications**:

**Remote Services from Home Jurisdiction**:
- **No physical presence**: Personnel working from home country generally don't create PE
- **Treaty position**: No PE under Article 5 (no fixed place, no personnel presence in source country)
- **Tax efficiency**: Avoids source taxation (Article 7 exclusive residence taxation if no PE)

**Exception - Equipment-Based PE**:
- **Remote monitoring equipment**: Sensors, SCADA systems, IoT devices physically in source country
- **Fixed place PE risk**: If equipment constitutes fixed place of business
- **Mitigation**: Equipment owned by client (service provider accesses remotely) avoids PE

**Withholding Tax Trend**:
- Some jurisdictions impose WHT on digital/remote services despite no physical presence
- **India**: Equalization levy on e-commerce services (bypasses treaty limitations)
- **Nigeria**: Expansion of WHT to digital services
- **Treaty override issues**: Conflict with Article 7 (no PE, no taxation)

**Planning Implications**:
- **Remote delivery**: Reduces PE risk but may not eliminate WHT exposure
- **Substance requirements**: Ensure genuine remote delivery (not disguised in-country presence)
- **Cost efficiency**: Lower mobilization costs make remote delivery attractive even with WHT

### 6.4 Transfer Pricing Documentation and Scrutiny

**OECD BEPS Actions 8-10 and 13**: Enhanced transfer pricing rules and documentation.

**Technical Services Focus**:
- **Shareholder services**: Increased scrutiny whether services provide genuine benefit to recipient
- **Value creation**: Service fees must align with value delivered
- **Benchmarking**: Requirement for robust comparability analysis

**Documentation Requirements** (Implemented 2018-2025):
- **Master File**: Group structure, transfer pricing policies, global allocation of income
- **Local File**: Detailed transaction-level analysis, including technical services
- **Country-by-Country Reporting (CbCR)**: Revenue, profits, taxes, employees by jurisdiction

**Energy Sector Audits**:
- Tax authorities increasingly challenging:
  - Excessive technical service fees (profit shifting)
  - Related party services lacking arm's length pricing
  - Insufficient value delivery (shareholder services)
- **Penalties**: Significant penalties for insufficient documentation or aggressive pricing

**Best Practices**:
- **Contemporaneous documentation**: Prepare before filing tax return
- **Functional analysis**: Clearly articulate functions, assets, risks
- **Benchmarking studies**: Identify comparable independent service providers
- **Intercompany agreements**: Written contracts specifying service scope and pricing

### 6.5 Increased Substance Requirements (BEPS Action 5)

**Focus**: Prevent treaty shopping through letterbox companies lacking genuine economic substance.

**Technical Services Hubs**: Regional hubs (Netherlands, Singapore, UAE, Cyprus) historically used to access favorable treaty networks.

**Substance Requirements** (OECD BEPS Action 5 Minimum Standards):
- **Adequate premises**: Office facilities commensurate with activities
- **Qualified employees**: Personnel with technical skills to perform services
- **Decision-making**: Strategic decisions made in hub jurisdiction
- **Economic activity**: Real business operations, not mere invoicing

**Tax Authority Challenges**:
- **Substance audits**: Examining whether hub has genuine substance
- **Beneficial ownership**: Identifying true economic owner for treaty purposes
- **Principal Purpose Test (PPT)**: Denying benefits if principal purpose was tax avoidance

**Impact on Service Companies**:
- **Increased costs**: Must maintain real operations in hub jurisdiction
- **Risk**: Treaty benefits denied if insufficient substance
- **Mitigation**: Genuine operational substance (employees, offices, client relationships)

### 6.6 UN Practical Manual on Transfer Pricing

**2021-2024 Updates**: UN Transfer Pricing Manual provides guidance relevant to developing countries.

**Technical Services Chapter**: Specific guidance on transfer pricing for services, including:
- **Low value-adding services**: Simplified approach (cost-plus 5%)
- **Shareholder services**: When services not deductible (benefit parent only)
- **Make-or-buy test**: Would independent party purchase service?
- **Intra-group services vs. shareholder activities**: Distinction framework

**Developing Country Perspective**: Manual reflects concerns about base erosion through service fees.

**Practical Impact**:
- Developing country tax authorities increasingly scrutinize service fees
- Lower acceptable markups for routine services (5-10% common)
- Higher burden of proof on taxpayer to demonstrate arm's length nature
