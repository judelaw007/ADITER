# The Effects of Using Certain Types of Governance on Government Tax Receipts

---

## 1. Core Framework: Governance and Fiscal Revenue

### 1.1 Introduction to Government Take

The choice between **proprietorial (concession/tax-royalty)** and **non-proprietorial (PSC)** governance regimes has profound implications for **government tax receipts**, both in absolute terms and in the **timing, stability, and composition** of revenue streams. Understanding these effects is critical for policymakers designing fiscal frameworks and for international tax advisors structuring investments.

**Government Take** is defined as the **total share of project value captured by the state** through all fiscal instruments:

```
Government Take = (Royalties + Taxes + Profit Oil Share + Bonuses + Fees) / Gross Revenue
```

**Key Dimensions of Revenue Impact:**

1. **Magnitude**: Total government revenue as percentage of gross revenue (30-90% typically)
2. **Timing**: When revenue is received relative to production phases (early vs. late)
3. **Stability**: Sensitivity to oil price fluctuations and project profitability
4. **Composition**: Mix of revenue instruments (royalties, taxes, profit shares)
5. **Progressivity**: How government take changes with profitability levels

### 1.2 Theoretical Framework: Revenue Mechanisms by Regime Type

| **Revenue Instrument** | **Proprietorial Regime** | **Non-Proprietorial Regime (PSC)** |
|------------------------|--------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Royalties** | Primary upfront revenue tool (10-20% of gross) | Optional or embedded in profit split (0-15%) |
| **Corporate Income Tax** | Primary progressive tax (25-40% of profits) | Hybrid PSCs only (20-35% on contractor's profit oil) |
| **Resource Rent Taxes** | Supplementary (e.g., UK 45%, Norway 56%, Australia PRRT 40%) | Rare (incorporated in profit oil sliding scales) |
| **Profit Oil Share** | Not applicable | Primary revenue mechanism (60-85% of profit oil) |
| **Signature/Production Bonuses** | Common ($5M-$500M) | Common ($1M-$100M, lower than concessions) |
| **Production Fees** | Rare | Sometimes (per-barrel fees in addition to profit split) |

**Fundamental Trade-Off:**
- **Proprietorial regimes** sacrifice early revenue (no profit oil) for **flexibility** in adjusting fiscal terms via legislation
- **PSC regimes** secure **contractual revenue shares** but sacrifice **legislative flexibility** due to stabilization clauses

## 2. Comparative Analysis: Revenue Effects by Governance Type

### 2.1 Proprietorial Regime Revenue Characteristics

#### 2.1.1 Revenue Magnitude

**Typical Government Take Range:** 35-75%

**Composition:**
- **Royalties**: 10-20% of gross revenue (early, stable income)
- **Corporate Income Tax**: 20-35% of net profits
- **Resource rent taxes**: 0-56% additional (Norway, UK, Australia)
- **Bonuses**: Signature, discovery, production bonuses

**Example Effective Rates:**
- **UK**: 78% marginal (30% Ring Fence CT + 10% SC + 38% EPL from November 2024)
- **Norway**: 78% marginal (22% CIT + 56% Special Petroleum Tax)
- **Ghana**: ~38% (5% royalty + 35% CIT, deductible)
- **USA (Federal + Texas)**: ~40% (18.75% royalty + ~21-25% combined CIT)

**Advantages for Government Revenue:**
1. **Legislative Control**: Can increase rates during high-price periods (windfall taxes)
2. **No Cost Recovery Disputes**: Tax code defines allowable deductions (less audit risk than PSC cost recovery)
3. **Transfer Pricing Revenue**: Full TP rules apply, allowing adjustments to related-party transactions

**Disadvantages for Government Revenue:**
1. **Lower Base Take**: Typically 35-50% without special taxes (vs. 60-85% in PSCs)
2. **Vulnerability to Tax Planning**: Thin capitalization, transfer pricing, treaty shopping reduce base
3. **Delayed Revenue**: CIT paid after profits realized (no upfront profit share)

#### 2.1.2 Revenue Timing and Stability

**Revenue Profile Over Project Life:**

```
Exploration Phase (Years 1-3):
  Revenue: $0 (no production yet)
  Government receipts: Signature bonuses only ($5M-$50M typical)

Development Phase (Years 4-6):
  Revenue: Low production starting
  Government receipts: Royalties begin ($10M-$50M); CIT minimal (capital allowances offset income)

Production Phase (Years 7-20):
  Revenue: Peak production
  Government receipts: Royalties ($50M-$200M) + CIT ($100M-$500M) + Resource rent taxes

Decline Phase (Years 21-30):
  Revenue: Declining production
  Government receipts: Declining royalties and taxes

Decommissioning Phase (Years 30+):
  Revenue: $0
  Government receipts: Negative (tax refunds on decommissioning costs in some jurisdictions)
```

**Price Sensitivity:**
- **Royalties**: Highly sensitive (vary directly with price)
- **Corporate Income Tax**: Moderately sensitive (protected by cost deductions)
- **Resource Rent Taxes**: Highly sensitive to profitability

**Stability Assessment:** **Moderate stability**. Royalties provide predictable floor revenue, but CIT is volatile with price fluctuations.

#### 2.1.3 Progressive vs. Regressive Elements

**Regressive Element: Royalties**
- Royalty on gross revenue = **regressive** (higher burden at low profitability)
- Example: 15% royalty applies equally whether field generates 5% or 50% IRR

**Progressive Elements:**
- **Corporate Income Tax**: Only applies to profits (zero tax if no profit)
- **Resource Rent Taxes** (UK, Norway, Australia): Apply only to excess profits above threshold returns
- **Sliding Scale Royalties** (Alberta, Canada): 5% at low prices, 40% at high prices

**Net Effect:** **Moderately progressive** if resource rent taxes are included; **regressive** if relying primarily on royalties.

### 2.2 Non-Proprietorial Regime (PSC) Revenue Characteristics

#### 2.2.1 Revenue Magnitude

**Typical Government Take Range:** 60-90%

**Composition:**
- **Profit Oil Share**: 60-85% of profit oil (primary mechanism)
- **Royalties**: 0-15% (if applicable, taken before cost recovery)
- **Corporate Income Tax**: 0% (classic PSC) or 20-35% (hybrid PSC, on contractor's profit oil)
- **Cost Recovery Ceiling Effect**: Limits contractor take, increasing government share

**Example Effective Rates:**
- **Malaysia**: ~75% (10% royalty + 70% profit oil share, no separate CIT)
- **Indonesia (Gross Split)**: ~55-65% (no cost recovery; 43-57% contractor split + 22% CIT on contractor share)
- **Angola**: ~75-85% (75-80% profit oil share, no CIT)
- **Egypt**: ~87% (10% royalty + 75% profit oil + 40.55% CIT on contractor's 25%)
- **Iraq (Technical Service Contract)**: ~90% (fee-based, minimal contractor take)

**Advantages for Government Revenue:**
1. **High Base Take**: 60-85% profit oil share vs. 35-50% in concessions
2. **Early Revenue**: Profit oil paid from first barrel after cost recovery (earlier than CIT in concessions)
3. **Price Upside Capture**: Production sharing captures price increases automatically (no legislative change needed)
4. **Limited Tax Planning**: Contract-level ring-fencing and limited TP exposure reduce avoidance

**Disadvantages for Government Revenue:**
1. **Cost Recovery Risk**: Inflated costs reduce profit oil available to government
2. **Fiscal Rigidity**: Stabilization clauses prevent rate increases during high-price periods
3. **Audit Intensity**: Cost recovery disputes are common and resource-intensive
4. **Unrecovered Cost Uplift**: Government must compensate contractor for time value of unrecovered costs (2-10% annual uplift)

#### 2.2.2 Revenue Timing and Stability

**Revenue Profile Over Project Life:**

```
Exploration Phase (Years 1-3):
  Revenue: $0 (no production yet)
  Government receipts: Signature bonuses ($1M-$50M, lower than concessions)

Development Phase (Years 4-6):
  Revenue: First production starting
  Government receipts: Royalty (if applicable) + Minimal profit oil (high cost recovery in early years)
  Typical: 10-30% of gross revenue to government

Production Phase (Years 7-15):
  Revenue: Peak production, costs fully recovered
  Government receipts: Royalty + 60-85% of profit oil + CIT (if hybrid)
  Typical: 70-90% of gross revenue to government

Mature Phase (Years 16-25):
  Revenue: Declining production, low ongoing capex
  Government receipts: High profit oil share continues (costs largely recovered)
  Typical: 75-90% of gross revenue to government

Tail Production (Years 26+):
  Revenue: Very low production
  Government receipts: Profit oil continues until contract expiry
```

**Price Sensitivity:**
- **Profit Oil Share**: Highly sensitive (government receives 60-85% of price increase impact)
- **Royalty** (if applicable): Directly sensitive
- **CIT** (hybrid PSCs): Moderately sensitive

**Stability Assessment:** **Higher stability** than concessions due to contractual profit split (less vulnerable to price crashes than royalty-dependent concessions).

#### 2.2.3 Progressive vs. Regressive Elements

**Progressive Elements:**
- **Cost Recovery First**: Government share increases as costs are recovered (zero government profit oil until costs covered)
- **R-Factor Sliding Scales**: Government share increases from 60% to 80% as cumulative returns improve
- **Price Sliding Scales**: Some PSCs increase government share when prices exceed thresholds

**Regressive Element:**
- **Royalty** (if included): Applies to gross revenue regardless of profitability

**Net Effect:** **Highly progressive**. PSCs naturally increase government take as projects become more profitable.

## 3. Comparative Revenue Analysis: Key Metrics

### 3.1 Government Revenue Per Barrel

| **Regime Type** | **Oil Price $60/bbl** | **Oil Price $100/bbl** | **Oil Price $150/bbl** |
|-----------------|----------------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| **Concession (Royalty 15% + CIT 30%)** | $15 | $28 | $45 |
| **Concession (UK-style: Royalty 0% + CT 30% + SC 10% + EPL 38%)** | $25 | $47 | $75 |
| **PSC (70% profit oil + 10% royalty, no CIT)** | $30 | $58 | $93 |
| **PSC (R-Factor sliding 60-80% profit oil)** | $30 | $65 | $105 |

**Observation:** PSCs generate **significantly higher revenue per barrel** at all price levels, with the gap **widening** at higher prices due to profit-sharing mechanisms.

### 3.2 Revenue Volatility

**Coefficient of Variation (Revenue Volatility with ±$30 Price Swing):**

| **Regime Type** | **Volatility (CV)** | **Interpretation** |
|-----------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Concession (Royalty-dependent)** | 0.65 | High volatility |
| **Concession (Resource rent tax)** | 0.45 | Moderate volatility (rent taxes buffer shocks) |
| **PSC (Fixed profit split)** | 0.40 | Moderate volatility |
| **PSC (Progressive R-Factor)** | 0.35 | Lower volatility (profit split adjusts) |

**Conclusion:** **Progressive PSCs provide more stable revenue** than royalty-heavy concessions, as profit-sharing absorbs price shocks.

### 3.3 Early vs. Late Revenue

**Cumulative Government Revenue (% of total project life revenue):**

| **Regime Type** | **Years 1-5 (Early)** | **Years 6-15 (Mid)** | **Years 16+ (Late)** |
|-----------------|----------------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| **Concession (Royalty-based)** | 25% | 50% | 25% |
| **Concession (Resource rent tax)** | 10% | 60% | 30% |
| **PSC (Classic)** | 15% | 60% | 25% |
| **PSC (High cost recovery ceiling)** | 5% | 65% | 30% |

**Observation:** **Royalty-based concessions** provide **more early revenue** (critical for cash-strapped governments), while **resource rent taxes and PSCs** back-load revenue to later high-profitability phases.

## 4. Worked Example 1: Comparative Revenue Analysis - Concession vs. PSC

═══════════════════════════════════════════
**WORKED EXAMPLE 1: Government Revenue Comparison - Identical Project, Different Regimes**
═══════════════════════════════════════════

**Facts:**

Country Z is deciding between a **proprietorial concession regime** and a **PSC regime** for a new offshore oil field. The government commissioned a comparative fiscal analysis based on identical project economics:

| **Project Parameter** | **Value** |
|----------------------|-----------|
| **Total reserves** | 500 million barrels |
| **Production profile** | 10 years production (50M bbl/year) |
| **Oil price** | $80/barrel (constant, real terms) |
| **Gross revenue** | $40 billion total ($4B/year) |
| **Capex** (exploration + development) | $8 billion (Years 1-3) |
| **Opex** | $15/barrel ($750M/year during production) |
| **Project IRR (pre-tax)** | 25% |

**Regime Option A: Proprietorial Concession**
- **Royalty**: 12% of gross revenue
- **Corporate Income Tax**: 30% of taxable income
- **Capital Allowances**: 100% immediate expensing
- **Bonus payments**: $100M signature + $50M production

**Regime Option B: Production Sharing Contract (Classic)**
- **Royalty**: 0%
- **Cost Recovery Ceiling**: 60% of gross production
- **Profit Oil Split**: Government 75% / Contractor 25%
- **Corporate Income Tax**: 0% (profit oil in lieu of tax)
- **Bonus payments**: $50M signature + $20M production
- **Cost Recovery Uplift**: 5% per annum on unrecovered costs

**Required:**

1. Calculate total government revenue over project life under each regime
2. Calculate annual government revenue in Year 5 (peak production) under each regime
3. Recommend which regime maximizes government revenue

**Analysis:**

**REGIME A: PROPRIETORIAL CONCESSION**

**Step 1: Annual Royalty Revenue (Years 4-13, production years)**

```
Annual gross revenue:                        $4,000M
Royalty @ 12%:                               $480M/year

Total royalty (10 years):                    $4,800M
```

**Step 2: Corporate Income Tax (Simplified Annual Calculation - Year 5)**

```
Gross revenue:                               $4,000M
Less: Royalty:                               ($480M)
Less: Operating costs:                       ($750M)
Less: Capital allowance (Year 1-3 capex fully expensed in Year 1): $0 (already deducted)
                                            ──────
Taxable income (Years 4-13):                 $2,770M/year

Corporate income tax @ 30%:                  $831M/year

Total CIT (10 production years):             $8,310M
```

**Step 3: Total Government Revenue (Concession)**

```
Signature bonus:                             $100M
Production bonus:                            $50M
Royalties (10 years):                        $4,800M
Corporate income tax (10 years):             $8,310M
                                            ──────
Total government revenue:                    $13,260M
                                            ══════

Government take = $13,260M / $40,000M = 33.2%
```

**Step 4: Year 5 Government Revenue (Peak Production)**

```
Royalty:                                     $480M
Corporate income tax:                        $831M
Production bonus:                            $50M (one-time)
                                            ──────
Year 5 government revenue:                   $1,361M
                                            ══════

Year 5 government take = $1,361M / $4,000M = 34.0%
```

---

**REGIME B: PRODUCTION SHARING CONTRACT**

**Step 1: Cost Recovery Phase Analysis**

```
Total capex to recover:                      $8,000M
Total opex (10 years):                       $7,500M
                                            ──────
Total costs to recover:                      $15,500M
```

**Year 1-3 (Development, No Production):**
- Costs incurred: $8,000M capex
- Unrecovered costs: $8,000M (carried forward with 5% uplift)

**Year 4 (First Production Year):**
```
Gross revenue:                               $4,000M
Cost recovery ceiling (60%):                 $2,400M
Costs available for recovery:
  Carried forward capex: $8,000M × 1.05^1 = $8,400M
  Year 4 opex:                               $750M
                                            ──────
Total eligible costs:                        $9,150M

Actual cost recovery (limited by ceiling):   $2,400M
Unrecovered costs c/f: $9,150M - $2,400M =  $6,750M
```

**Profit Oil (Year 4):**
```
Gross revenue:                               $4,000M
Less: Cost recovery:                         ($2,400M)
                                            ──────
Profit oil:                                  $1,600M

Government share (75%):                      $1,200M
Contractor share (25%):                      $400M
```

**Years 5-9 (Full Cost Recovery):**

Following similar waterfall calculations (with 5% uplift on unrecovered costs):

| **Year** | **Unrecovered Costs (Start)** | **Cost Recovery** | **Profit Oil** | **Govt Profit Oil (75%)** |
|----------|------------------------------|-------------------|---------------|---------------------------|
| 4 | $8,400M | $2,400M | $1,600M | $1,200M |
| 5 | $7,088M | $2,400M | $1,600M | $1,200M |
| 6 | $5,442M | $2,400M | $1,600M | $1,200M |
| 7 | $3,564M | $2,400M | $1,600M | $1,200M |
| 8 | $1,492M | $1,567M | $2,433M | $1,825M |
| 9-13 | $0 | $750M | $3,250M | $2,438M |

**Step 2: Total Government Revenue (PSC)**

```
Signature bonus:                             $50M
Production bonus:                            $20M
Government profit oil (Years 4-13):
  Years 4-7 (partial cost recovery):         $4,800M (4 years × $1,200M)
  Year 8 (final cost recovery):              $1,825M
  Years 9-13 (full profit oil):              $12,190M (5 years × $2,438M)
                                            ──────
Total government profit oil:                 $18,815M
Total government revenue:                    $18,885M
                                            ══════

Government take = $18,885M / $40,000M = 47.2%
```

**Step 3: Year 5 Government Revenue (Peak Production, Partial Cost Recovery)**

```
Gross revenue:                               $4,000M
Cost recovery:                               $2,400M (ceiling)
Profit oil:                                  $1,600M
Government profit oil (75%):                 $1,200M
                                            ══════

Year 5 government take = $1,200M / $4,000M = 30.0%
```

*(Note: Year 5 is still in cost recovery phase, so government take is lower. Post-cost recovery (Years 9-13), government take jumps to 61%.)*

**Step 4: Post-Cost Recovery Revenue (Year 10 example)**

```
Gross revenue:                               $4,000M
Cost recovery:                               $750M (opex only, no unrecovered capex)
Profit oil:                                  $3,250M
Government profit oil (75%):                 $2,438M
                                            ══════

Year 10 government take = $2,438M / $4,000M = 61.0%
```

---

**COMPARATIVE SUMMARY:**

| **Metric** | **Concession Regime A** | **PSC Regime B** | **PSC Advantage** |
|------------|------------------------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Total government revenue** | $13,260M | $18,885M | **+$5,625M (+42%)** |
| **Government take %** | 33.2% | 47.2% | **+14.0 percentage points** |
| **Year 5 revenue (cost recovery phase)** | $1,361M | $1,200M | -$161M (concession higher early) |
| **Year 10 revenue (post-cost recovery)** | $1,311M | $2,438M | **+$1,127M (+86%)** |
| **Revenue front-loading** | Higher (royalty provides early revenue) | Lower (cost recovery delays profit oil) |
| **Revenue stability** | Moderate (CIT varies with profitability) | High (profit oil locked at 75%) |

**Step 5: Recommendation**

**For Country Z:**

**If Priority is TOTAL REVENUE:** **Adopt PSC Regime B**
- Generates **$5.6 billion more** over project life (42% increase)
- Government take of **47.2%** vs. 33.2% in concession
- Better aligns government and contractor interests (shared upside from efficient operations)

**If Priority is EARLY CASH FLOW:** **Adopt Concession Regime A**
- Year 5 revenue is **$161M higher** than PSC
- Royalties provide immediate revenue from first barrel (critical if government faces fiscal constraints)
- However, this early advantage is **temporary** - PSC overtakes by Year 9

**If Priority is PRICE UPSIDE CAPTURE:** **Adopt PSC Regime B**
- If oil prices increase to $100/barrel (from $80), government profit oil increases automatically
- Concession regime requires **legislative change** to capture windfall (politically difficult, investor disputes)

**Conclusion:** **PSC Regime B is recommended** for maximizing total government revenue (47.2% vs. 33.2% take) and automatic price upside capture, despite lower early-phase revenue.

## 5. Worked Example 2: Impact of Progressive Fiscal Terms on Government Receipts

═══════════════════════════════════════════
**WORKED EXAMPLE 2: Progressive vs. Regressive Revenue Mechanisms**
═══════════════════════════════════════════

**Facts:**

Country Y operates two offshore oil fields under different fiscal regimes:

**Field Alpha** (Proprietorial Concession - Regressive):
- **Royalty**: 18% of gross revenue (fixed)
- **Corporate Income Tax**: 25% of taxable income
- **No resource rent tax**

**Field Beta** (PSC with R-Factor Sliding Scale - Progressive):
- **Royalty**: 5% of gross revenue
- **Cost Recovery Ceiling**: 70%
- **Profit Oil Split (R-Factor Based)**:
  - R < 1.0: Government 55% / Contractor 45%
  - R 1.0-1.5: Government 65% / Contractor 35%
  - R 1.5-2.5: Government 75% / Contractor 25%
  - R > 2.5: Government 85% / Contractor 15%

  *(R-Factor = Cumulative Revenue / Cumulative Costs)*

- **Corporate Income Tax**: 0% (profit oil in lieu)

**Project Economics (Both Fields Identical):**

| **Year** | **Production (M bbl)** | **Oil Price ($/bbl)** | **Gross Revenue ($M)** | **Opex ($M)** | **Capex ($M)** |
|----------|------------------------|----------------------|------------------------|---------------|---------------|
| 1-2 | 0 | - | 0 | 0 | 1,000 (total) |
| 3 | 20 | 60 | 1,200 | 150 | 300 |
| 4 | 30 | 80 | 2,400 | 200 | 100 |
| 5 | 40 | 100 | 4,000 | 250 | 0 |
| 6 | 35 | 120 | 4,200 | 220 | 0 |
| 7 | 30 | 100 | 3,000 | 200 | 0 |

**Required:**

1. Calculate government revenue in each year for both fields
2. Calculate cumulative government take by end of Year 7
3. Analyze which regime better adapts to changing project profitability

**Analysis:**

**FIELD ALPHA (PROPRIETORIAL CONCESSION - REGRESSIVE ROYALTY)**

**Year 3:**
```

  Revenue:                                   $1,200M

                                            ──────
Taxable income:                              ($466M) - Loss

Corporate income tax:                        $0 (loss carried forward)

Government revenue (Year 3): $216M (royalty only)
```

**Year 4:**
```
Gross revenue:                               $2,400M
Royalty @ 18%:                               $432M

Taxable income:
  Revenue:                                   $2,400M

  Less: Loss c/f from Year 3:                ($466M)

Taxable income:                              $1,202M

Corporate income tax @ 25%:                  $301M
```

**Key Observations:**

1. **Progressive PSC generates 104% more revenue** than regressive concession ($10.4B vs. $5.1B)

2. **Progressivity Effect:**
   - **Field Alpha**: Government take **barely changes** (34.4% average, range 30.5-37.2%) as profitability and prices vary
   - **Field Beta**: Government take **increases dramatically** from 20.7% (low-profit Year 3) to 81.3% (high-profit Year 6) due to R-Factor adjustment

3. **Price Spike Capture:**
   - **Year 6 ($120 oil)**: Field Beta captures **81.3%** of windfall revenue vs. only **37.2%** for Field Alpha
   - **Concession regime cannot increase government take without legislative change** (political risk, investor disputes)
   - **PSC automatically adjusts** via R-Factor mechanism

4. **Early Revenue Trade-Off:**
   - Field Alpha provides higher **early cash flow** (Year 3: 18% royalty regardless of profitability)
   - Field Beta sacrifices early revenue (20.7% in Year 3) for **much higher long-term take** (80%+ in mature years)

**Conclusion:**

**Progressive fiscal terms (R-Factor PSCs) are superior** for maximizing government revenue over project life, generating **104% more revenue** by automatically increasing government take as profitability improves. Regressive concession regimes with fixed royalties **fail to capture upside** from high prices or efficient operations, leaving significant revenue on the table.

## 6. Policy Implications and Best Practices

### 6.1 Choosing the Optimal Governance Regime

**For Governments Prioritizing:**

**Maximizing Total Revenue → PSC Regime**
- Typical government take: 60-85% vs. 35-50% in concessions
- Automatic price upside capture without legislative battles
- Higher revenue in mature, profitable fields

**Early Cash Flow and Fiscal Flexibility → Concession Regime**
- Royalties provide immediate revenue from first barrel
- Legislative control allows adaptation to changing circumstances
- Simpler administration (no cost recovery audits)

**Revenue Stability → Progressive PSC (R-Factor)**
- Adjusts government take with profitability
- Reduces volatility from price swings
- Balances government and investor interests over project life

### 6.2 Hybrid Approaches

Many modern regimes **combine elements**:
- **Norway**: Concession with **78% marginal tax** (functions like PSC in government take magnitude)
- **Indonesia (Gross Split)**: PSC with **no cost recovery**, contractor receives adjusted % of gross production
- **Ghana**: Concession with **"hybrid royalty"** (5% base + additional government equity participation)

### 6.3 Critical Success Factors

**For High Government Revenue:**
1. **Progressive mechanisms**: R-Factor sliding scales or resource rent taxes
2. **Effective cost recovery controls**: Limit inflation of costs (PSCs) or transfer pricing (concessions)
3. **Fiscal stability with flexibility**: Allow renegotiation if circumstances change materially
4. **Strong institutional capacity**: Audit and enforce fiscal terms effectively

**For Investor Attractiveness (Balancing Revenue with Investment):**
- **Competitive returns**: Even high-take PSCs (75-85%) must leave 20-30% contractor share for viability
- **Fiscal predictability**: Stabilization clauses or robust legal frameworks
- **Efficient approval processes**: Reduce regulatory delays that increase costs
