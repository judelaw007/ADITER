Understanding Tax Fundamentals

Tax forms the foundation of government funding and economic policy. To be an effective tax professional, you must understand not just the rules, but the principles underlying them.

Key Principle:

Income is generally defined as all gains, increases in net worth, and economic benefits received by a taxpayer during the tax year, unless specifically excluded by law.

Income Classification

  • Gross Income: All income received in the form of money, property, or services that is not specifically excluded from taxation
  • Ordinary Income: Income from regular business or employment activities taxed at regular rates
  • Capital Gains: Income from the sale of capital assets, potentially taxed at preferential rates
  • Passive Income: Income from investments, rental properties, and other non-active sources
  • Business Income: Profit from operating a trade or business
Example

Sarah, a consultant, earned $120,000 in consulting fees, received $8,000 in interest income, and sold stock with a long-term capital gain of $15,000. Her gross income is $143,000. She may have deductions to reduce this to taxable income.

Deductions and Credits

Type Purpose Examples
Above-the-Line Reduce gross income IRA contributions, student loan interest, business losses
Below-the-Line Reduce taxable income Itemized/standard deductions, dependent exemptions
Tax Credits Dollar-for-dollar tax reduction Earned Income Credit, Child Tax Credit, Education Credits
Key Distinction

A $5,000 deduction might save $1,250 in taxes (at 25% rate), but a $5,000 credit saves exactly $5,000 in taxes. This is why credits are more valuable than deductions.
